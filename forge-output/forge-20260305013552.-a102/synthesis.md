# Source A vs Source B: Synthesis

## Source A Overview
Source A is a macroeconomic evidence brief grounded in FRED data for the U.S. economy. It uses three formal time-series indicators—CPIAUCSL, UNRATE, and FEDFUNDS—along with a policy reference page from the Federal Reserve.

### Core characteristics
- Theme: macroeconomic trend analysis across inflation, labor, and monetary policy.
- Data design: downloaded CSV extracts and derived annual summaries.
- Time window: historical range covers 2021–2024 with latest observations through early 2026.
- Output style: analytical report with quantified tables, calculated percentages, and explicit methodological notes.

### Main message
The source argues that the economy appears in a late-cycle normalization phase: inflation has declined from earlier peaks, unemployment has risen moderately, and policy rates have started to ease after a restrictive period.

## Source B Overview
Source B is a concise field-notes memo about engineering team operations. It is based on interviews and ticket sampling from January–February 2026 in mid-size teams.

### Core characteristics
- Theme: software team execution and incident-management effectiveness.
- Data design: qualitative observations and working hypotheses.
- Time window: a narrow 2-month operational window.
- Output style: short operational memo with bullets and action prompts.

### Main message
The source suggests execution speed improves with practical runbooks, explicit ownership for alerts, and recurring review rituals; it recommends additional validation and quantification of response-time outcomes.

## Direct comparison

### 1) Purpose and audience
- Source A targets decision-makers in finance and planning, providing macro indicators for scenario framing.
- Source B targets engineering leaders and operators, focusing on team process quality and reliability workflows.

### 2) Evidence basis
- Source A relies on measurable series and reproducible calculations.
- Source B relies on interview and ticket-log patterns, then proposes next tests.

### 3) Level of rigor
- Source A includes source-grounded computations and explicit data limits.
- Source B is exploratory and directional, with hypotheses that remain to be validated.

### 4) Time sensitivity
- Source A includes trend evidence over multiple years and a few “latest” points.
- Source B is a short, recent snapshot intended to trigger further validation.

### 5) Actionability
- Source A supports policy, risk, or budget assumptions through conditional scenarios.
- Source B supports process improvement through runbook redesign, ownership mapping, and incident cadence.

## Synthesis conclusion
Source A and Source B are complementary in scope rather than directly overlapping: Source A is macro-data analysis for high-level economic interpretation, while Source B is operational behavior research for team-level performance. Their findings can be combined if one wants to connect environment-level assumptions (for example, inflation and cost-of-capital context from Source A) with team reliability execution (from Source B), but each source would be weak if used alone for that cross-domain purpose.
